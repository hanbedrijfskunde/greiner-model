The Six Phases of Growth

Larry E. Greiner initially proposed five phases of growth in 1972, later adding a sixth in 1998. The core premise is that the very management practices that drive success in one phase eventually cause a crisis, necessitating a revolution to enter the next phase. Explore the phases below.

Growth through Creativity

Initial phase dominated by the founders, focusing on creating both a product and a market. Communication is frequent and informal.

Current Scientific Status & Criticisms

While Greiner's model remains a staple in MBA programs for its heuristic value, modern organizational science has heavily scrutinized and expanded upon it. Today's dynamic, digitally-driven ecosystems challenge the model's strict linearity.

1

The Linearity Critique

Historically, growth was viewed as sequential. Current research (e.g., studying tech startups and "Blitzscaling") shows organizations often skip phases entirely or experience multiple crises simultaneously due to hyper-growth.

2

Rise of Agile & Networks

Greiner's model assumes structural hierarchy. Modern management theory emphasizes Agile methodologies and holacracy, where the "Crisis of Control" is preempted by decentralized, autonomous teams operating with high alignment.

3

The Ecosystem Expansion

The 1998 addition of "Phase 6: Alliances" has become the focal point of current research. Growth is no longer bounded by the firm's walls, but relies on open innovation, platforms, and external ecosystem integration.

Summary of Current Consensus

Greiner’s underlying philosophy—that "the solutions to today's problems become the causes of tomorrow's crises"—remains scientifically robust and universally applicable. However, the specific structural prescriptions (Phases 1-5) are now viewed as highly dependent on industry context, technological leverage, and macroeconomic conditions rather than inevitable laws of physics.
